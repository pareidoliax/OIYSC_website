Visit during May 2001

Gianne Broughton and Nancy Drozd of CFSL visited our partners in Sierra Leone in early May, 2001. Here is Gianne's report of her impressions of some of the training programs they visited. An edited version of the trip report is also available for your information. 
 
 
Sierra Leonean Organizations Building a Culture of Peace
from Gianne Broughton 


In early May, 2001, Nancy Drozd and I observed culture-building in process first hand. Using an impressive toolkit of inter-personal communication skills with flare and art, project workers provoked and cajoled and listened as people analysed their own experience, began to understand alternatives, and came to believe that they could build peace. In the first stage, a group of about 20 men and women learn how to train community peace workers. We saw the opening day of training at the Council of Churches of Sierra Leone for people from the Makeni area in the northern region of the country that was still under rebel control. Three pairs of participants volunteered to try to push each other to opposite sides of the room: kind of a full-body arm-wrestling match. Amid laughter and grimaces of effort, participants observed their own responses too this controlled mini-conflict. Then, the facilitator lead people in an analysis of their responses and invited discussion of how they had seen similar responses in the life-and-death conflicts of their recent history. Everyone present could relate personal stories of how bloodshed was avoided or stopped. This group had four more days ahead of them in which they would learn skills in conflict analysis and resolution, trauma healing, community animation, and, most importantly, how to teach these skills to others. 

In In the second stage, which we observed for a group from Freetown at the YMCA, members from the original group of twenty had gone out and recruited 72 formal and informal community leaders from two neighbouring areas of the city by talking with people about who they go to for advice and assistance, or who they know is doing something for the community. These 72 were then trained by members of the original group using similar techniques and a curriculum that they had worked out in the first stage. 

Finally, in the third stage, these community leaders use their new or strengthened community animation skills to organise events and activities to include the whole community so that the conflicts and challenges facing the community can be discussed openly. We were able to see one such event organised by project trainees in a rural community near Bo, Semobu Folobu. With the help of a role-play, members of all segments of the community, including the civil militia (called "kamajors") who have a traditional role in Sierra Leone culture, discussed how disarmed rebels could be received back into the community. This seemed to be the primary challenge to further progress in the peace process. The rebels would not disarm and disband while they were certain that they would be retaliated against if they left the "bush" and returned to a settled life. For me, the role of war-time atrocities as a strategy for ensuring that a society remains in turmoil, was unmasked by this discussion. Traditional judicial processes were depicted, and a traditional libation ceremony and communal meal were also part of the day's activities. The sense we had of a community ready to face its future in a common search for peace was celebrated by traditional singing and dancing at the end of the day.
