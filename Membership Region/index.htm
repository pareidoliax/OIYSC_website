  • RENEWAL 2000 CONFERENCE REPORT - Central Canada Region
In November 2000, club representatives in Central Canada region met to discuss how to renew club strength in the region. Prior to the conference a survey of clubs produced the following list of concerns in order of priority.
  • Not recruiting enough members
  • Low level of community recognition and inadequate promotional work
  • Lack of proper training
  • Not enough communication between clubs
  • Lack of Y support
  • Problems in keeping members
The group worked on four questions dealing with the #1 priority, recruiting and keeping members.

What do we have to offer existing and prospective members?

How can we strengthen these aspects of our clubs?

Is what we have to offer meeting the needs of the members and prospectives?

If not, what else should we be looking at?

Four clear directions emerged from the discussion.

  • Each club needs to offer a wider variety of opportunities for involvement incorporating more group activities, clear objectives, specific time commitments.
  • Clubs need to stress out international focus by increasing our understanding of current issues, supporting the projects of other clubs and supporting directly the work of clubs in developing countries.
  • Clubs must present an open invitation to men and women of all ages, ethnic groups and faiths and include the whole family.
  • Clubs must work to build a fellowship based on respect, tolerance, equality and support for individual members while operating in a responsible, professional manner.
Within each area the group came up with some specific suggestions for club action
 
  • OPPORTUNITIES FOR INVOLVEMENT.
Under this topic, we set out three guidelines.

1.Clubs need to provide a wider range of activities to meet individual needs and skills of existing and prospective members.

2. Clubs need to provide as many group activities as possible to build fellowship and involve new people.

3. As much as possible, activities should be short-term with a definite time frame and clear objectives. Four general recommendations were developed with several ideas for implementation.
 

A. Use the overall international theme "Say No To Violence" as an impetus for more member involvement and closer co-operation with the Y and other community groups.

Possible we need to add to the theme a phrase such as " Build a Culture of Peace" and develop ideas for projects in each local community as well as internationally.

A big step would be to set up programs of awareness of the issues, in the Y and in the community.

Identify and support local community initiatives. Members choose which ones they might take part in and in turn invite others to participate.
 

B. Determine what we have to offer as a club and determine ways to expand the options. First review existing service and fund raising projects and outline the needs we have for volunteers and donations.

Get a complete list of Y projects where the club might be able to offer assistance, their "wish list"

Explore the possibility of assisting other agencies in the community who have similar objectives to ours. Members could provide volunteer help in "hands on" work and in organization, with Children's Aid Societies, shelters for the homeless, Food Banks and many more.

The main idea coming from the discussions was that we do not always need to develop and organize more new projects but to plug into already established projects, thus reducing the strain on our present human resources. This requires a good knowledge of what is happening in your community already. Also there will be more opportunities to approach people in the community for help in our work which may also lead to finding new members. We will be able to do more service with the same number of members.
 

C. Find out what people might be looking for in a Y Service club. An obvious target group for a random survey would be Y users using a professionally developed questionnaire. Every club would use the same survey and share results. Possibly set up a booth in the Y lobby at different times to sample different groups of Y users. Offer an incentive for completing the survey, e.g. entry into a draw for a free Christmas tree. Do the survey as part of an already planned event. An alternative might be to include some questions in the Y's registration material or newsletter. Keep it short. We need to decide what we want to find out and what we want to tell people about our movement.
 

D. We must share information with other clubs and with YMCAs to stimulate more ideas and to support each others initiatives. Later under the topic of International Outreach some specific examples of possible inter-club co-operation were put forward.

Using electronic communications we should be able to keep our members and the YMCAs in Central Canada informed of our work.
 
 
 

  • BUILDING OUR INTERNATIONAL EMPHASIS
There are some of our present international projects that deserve to be promoted to prospective members in more detail. Perhaps a key group we want to target for membership are people already interested in international issues around peace, development, understanding and justice.
 

A. Stress the opportunities available for exchanges and international contacts starting with the STEP program for young people since there continue to be difficulties in expanding the YEEP program.

Brother clubs in developing countries offer possibilities for direct support to worthwhile projects.

Support existing exchange programs for Y staff and volunteers.

Have all regional clubs support the special programs that some of our clubs have already started. For example:

Cambridge is involved in a youth exchange program between Canada, The U.S. and Mexico called "Mano a Mano" Clubs with interested young people can plug into this program.

North York has links with an organization, Friends of the Poor, that sends work parties to countries like Jamaica to help in building houses and schools in poor areas. They would welcome members of other clubs and volunteers from other Y's who would like to go.

Ottawa has helped develop a major project in Sierra Leone and is raising funds there directly as a result of their Brother Club relationship with Freetown Y Service club.

Sault Ste Marie has contacts with an "adopt a child" project in Sri Lanka that is being run by a Y Service club there. There may be other such club activities around the world where we can offer support or where we can participate directly.

The principle here is that if another club has a good project which members of our clubs might wish to support, make sure the opportunity is there and the information available. We need to gather as much information as possible and make sure every club has it.

We need to establish closer links with Y Canada International Development staff in Toronto.

Clubs can develop one time international projects such as an evening of letter writing, to Brother Clubs, for Amnesty International or for urging government action on important international issues such as debt cancellation.

We need to take the leadership in our Y's in developing awareness of issues with displays, speakers and activities with youth.
 
 
 

  • TOWARDS A MORE OPEN, INVITING FELLOWSHIP
A. Build bridges between our clubs and different ethnic groups in the community.

Support other ethnic associations in their charitable work with volunteers and expertise.
Support Y projects that reach out to ethnic groups and minorities.
Have joint meetings with community ethnic groups, celebrations, provide speakers for each others groups.
Carry out visible international projects which local ethnic groups can support. For example Ottawa's project in Sierra Leone has brought them together with the Sierra Leone Descendants Union.
 

B. Provide a more inviting milieu for single professional women (and men?)

Be more professional, in the conduct of meetings, in the treatment of other members, in the production and use of publicity material, in the operation of fundraising events.
Have clear and specific goals for the club mission. Set out specific time commitments for activities and stick to them. Free time is scarce and people want to be able to help by using their available time productively and enjoyably.
Invite people to help in a specific way, using their talents and interests appropriately.
Consider special community projects that might recognize people in the community for their contributions with a special award. These could provide needed club exposure and give you a reason to involve professionals in business and other fields.
 

C. Provide opportunities for seniors and retirees

Accessible facilities and activities.
Use their talents properly at the level of involvement they can handle.
Give opportunities to contribute, using the special knowledge and expertise that comes form a life of experience.
 
 
 

  • BUILD ON OUR CHRISTIAN BASE.
Point out our commonalities with all major religions
Maintain close contacts with churches of members and other faith groups such as the Jubilee Coalition.
Clubs could worship together once a year in a members church.
Celebrate events in the Christian calendar in the Y - carol singing.
Get the Y to provide a "prayer" room. Help set it up.
A Remembrance Day cross in the lobby.
People with a strong faith may be our best source of long time members.

  • RECOMMENDATIONS FOR CLUB ACTION - from the Renewal 2000 conference


EXPANDING OPPORTUNITIES FOR MEMBER INVOLVEMENT.

1. Review and evaluate existing community and international service projects. Is this the best mix to keep your members active and enthusiastic?
Do you have enough variety to attract new people? If you need to make improvements here are some ideas.

 2. Work with your Y to set up a list of their projects you can support, spread them out through they year and chose those that fit the needs and abilities of your club members.

3. Include in your service outreach, support for community groups with whom you have ideals and objectives in common. Instead of just writing a cheque, look at how individual members can get involved with these groups, as representatives of your club with full club support.

4. Find out what other clubs in the Region/Area are doing and look at ways to help them.

5. When you look at supporting Y and community projects, plug into International, Area and Regional themes, especially "Say No To Violence". Such unifying themes identify the club as a special group in the community.

6. Set up a communications strategy that shares your successes (and failures) with other clubs as well as Y staff, club contacts and other community groups.

7. Survey the public, starting with Y users, to find out what they would look for in a Y Service club which they might consider joining.
 

BUILDING A MORE OPEN, INVITING FELLOWSHIP.
 

1. Evaluate all aspects of meetings and programs which affect the first impressions of any guests. Are we as professional as possible in our actions and operations? Do we really welcome people of all ages and ethnic backgrounds? Are we attractive to professional people, especially women and  do we really accomodate the whole family? If not, decide on what changes you need to make.

2. Share with other clubs what works for you and find out what other clubs are doing.
 

EXPANDING YPOUR INTERNATIONAL INVOLVEMENT

1. Take your existing international projects and look at how to expand them to involve more people in the community. Look at STEP, Brother Club partnerships and joint projects, Time of Fast, any programs that would benefit from or interest community and Y volunteers and Y staff.

2. Look at other international projects operated by other Y Service clubs around the world. Offer your members the opportunity to become involved.

3. Look at projects of other international organizations and support interested members who wish to be involved on behalf of the club.

4. Take on programs to raise awareness of issues within the Y community.
 

MAKING OUR CHRISTIAN FAITH A MORE VITAL PART OF CLUB LIFE.

Evaluate the role of Christianity in your service and fellowship.
How can we be true to the teachings of Jesus Christ? How can we build a fellowship that is Christ-centred?
Can we establish closer links with other faith groups in the community and share resources and inspiration?

  • RECRUITING TIPS
(How to invite someone to join the club and have a reasonable chance they will come to at least one meeting.)

New members don't just appear, they have to be invited to a meeting. Preparing to deliver that invitation seems to be difficult for many members for several reasons :

- some do not feel comfortable asking others to join in case the invitation is rejected.

- some do not know how to go about raising the subject and need assurance that whatever the result, a friendship is not in jeopardy.

- some feel they are just not salespersons.

Here are a few tips to help you overcome your misgivings and make that first invitation.

REMEMBER YOU ARE NOT DOING THIS FOR YOURSELF BUT FOR THE PEOPLE IN OUR COMMUNITY WHO WILL BENEFIT FROM OUR HELP!

Think of the benefits to your club and your fellowship together!!
The larger the club membership, the more service work we can do. More members usually increases the opportunities for fellowship, for making new friends and for personal growth. You could also enjoy the club more with more people to share the load.

BE REALISTIC AND DON'T EXPECT TOO MUCH!

Operate on the 20% rule. If you ask 10 people to join you may get 2 to come to a meeting. You can greatly improve these odds if you plan ahead and use some of the ideas outlined here.
 

THINK IN TERMS OF A THREE STEP PROCESS.

Scouting the Field     Laying the groundwork         Issuing the invitation.

STEP 1. Scouting the Field.

Whenever you meet someone new, an early thought should be- "Would this person make a good club member?"

Keep a mental list and add people who have the values, personality and background that appeals to you. Make an effort to get to know people who:

- are in your church or are already involved in the community
- have young people who might benefit from our youth exchanges
- are facing or who have just started retirement
- are experiencing the "empty nest" syndrome where their children have all left home.

Find out about jobs, family, community involvement, interests and hobbies. Look for a fit between their interests and the work of the club and start talking up Y'sdom.

STEP 2. Laying the Groundwork.

Be proud of your membership. Whenever you get the chance, talk about the club, its projects and your involvement. The best candidates for an invitation are those who ask about your involvement and show an interest in the clubs work. IF YOU ARE LUCKY THEY WILL ASK FOR MORE INFORMATION WITHOUT PROMPTING.

If someone shows an interest in a particular area, you can invite them to help in a specific event, a service project or fund raising event, such as selling Christmas Trees, collecting stamps, contributing to a sale.

Start keeping a record.

After some initial contacts, you may decide someone is worth inviting to a meeting. To make the invitation attractive you need to find out the values and interests of that person, such things as those outlined in our induction ceremony - Idealism, international outlook, enthusiastic, supportive of the Y , responsible and with a strong spiritual belief.

With a list by your telephone you will be reminded to keep in touch and look for the opportunity to make that invitation.

STEP 3. Issuing the invitation.

Timing is everything

Surveys show that the best time to recruit people for volunteer work is late summer to early fall when people are looking for something new to do. The next best time is around Christmas and the New Year.

Another consideration is the stage in a person's life as when :

  • Young people are just finished university and have their first job.
  • Young couples have their children now in school.
  • Middle-aged couples have children who have just left home.
  • Recent retirees have extra time to get involved.


Keep a list.

Every so often, especially during the summer, sit down and review the list of people you have met during the past year. Set some priorities and concentrate on those who have something in common with the ideals of the club.
 

Choose the right activity for the invitation

You have to decide which club event will be the best for you to bring your guest.

- a regular dinner which is more formal but hopefully informative and fun.
- a social occasion where your guest can meet others informally.
- a project where most of the club are involved.

Be aware of the process for becoming a member and the resources the club has available.

We do not want to rush someone into membership but also we cannot drag the process out. A prospective member should be able to determine in 2-3 months whether he/she is interested in making a commitment. In that time ask people to attend at least three meetings before making a decision. There may also be an orientation session and of course the club bulletin is sent out to them and they are put on the phoning list.

Clubs should have - a Guest Pak for their first meeting

                            - a Club Membership booklet for the orientation night.

                            - a New Member Kit for the Induction Ceremony
 

The best resource for a new member is YOU, the sponsor. Your responsibilities as a recruiter do not end with an induction though everyone in the club is expected to join in and support a new member. Many clubs assign a "buddy" to help the sponsor keep up the regular contact. Surveys indicate that member dropout occurs usually in their first two years. It is a lot easier to keep a member than find a new one but it still needs effort.

  • WHAT YOUR CLUB CAN DO !!!  Ideas from the Canada-U.S. Area Convention in Toledo, July, 1999.
  1. Make clubs more open and inclusive:
 - involve the whole family in activities
 - actively recruit women
 - consider subsidizing membership fees
 - involve youth in service early
 - respond to the needs of parents.
 - set targets for recruitment
 - be more flexible in meetings and programs
 - less formality, more fun, fellowship and spontaneity.

2. Work with and listen to youth.
 - start a youth section
 - start a second club for younger people
 - work with or adopt existing youth groups, in the Y, churches, schools.
 - carry out high profile work with young people or in areas youth relate to.
 - think young, listen to and educate youth.

3. Establish incentives, remove impediments to getting and keeping members.
 - create a flexible dues structure with youth and family memberships
 - provide more opportunities for personal development.
 - provide more flexibility in time commitments
 - more hands-on, group service opportunities.
 - broaden the focus of service to accommodate different interests.
 - be open to new ideas
 - have a clear focus or mission.

4. Develop among the members a feeling that they belong to a larger, world-wide family.
 - more interclub visits and communication.
 - more sharing of information and ideas among clubs
 - improve intra-club communication using electronic technology

5. Stress our service involvement and increase service opportunities for members.
 - more community service in general
 - hands-on projects that involve the whole family and friends
 - keep abreast of changing community needs
 - develop service projects as vehicles for recruitment
 - have projects that involve and support youth

6. Develop a positive, recognizable image of Y'sdom in our communities.
 - greater involvement in our communities
 - collaborate with other community groups as partners
 - support regional and national projects with other clubs.
 - publicize during fundraising, how funds are used and where.
 - make better use of the media in all forms.
 - continue to work on improving two-way communications with your local Y

 Most of these ideas are not new but may need a different approach in implementation.
We need to  find the innovative clubs who have tried new ways and are succeeding in building
their club, in morale, in community recognition, in support from their Y and ultimately in
membership. If you have had success in any of these areas, let me know and I will share your success with others.
