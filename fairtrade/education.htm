Building a Fair Trade Community.

What does it mean to become and remain a Fair Trade Community?

Suggestions for YMCAs, schools, churches and other community groups
There are four steps:

  1. Adopt a Statement of Principle. This may be provided by the national organization or a local administrative unit such as a school board. (A sample motion is provided)
  2. Establish an Ethical Purchasing policy that matches the needs and resources of your particular organization, to be implemented wherever possible, recognizing the constraints of the budget and the increased costs that may result.

    3. Include products that are certified Fair Trade, are Sweat-free and that are produced and used without harming the environment. Include a provision to avoid buying from companies deemed to be getting their goods from companies that include sweatshop labour, children as workers, poor and unsafe working conditions and lax environmental controls. Follow an ethical investment policy for all funds.
  3. Develop an ongoing campaign of awareness of the issues primarily among your members and contacts.   Discuss fully with the membership the rationale for ethical purchasing and the costs associated with such a decision.
    • Host visiting Fair Trade producers. Such tours are organized by groups such as Transfair Canada and Oxfam.
    • Purchase Fair Trade items such as coffee, tea, chocolate for special functions and everyday use in cafeterias, coffee machines and snack bars.
    • Make available to the staff and membership Fair Trade items for purchase. Selections of items are available from groups such as Bridgehead and Coco Camino for gifts and for fund raising campaigns.
    • Carry items in newsletters about latest news of Fair Trade products and actions by other groups in support of No Sweat and Fair Trade.
    • Display and make available any lists of Fair Trade companies in your area and encourage members to shop there.
  1. Take specific actions to promote Fair Trade to the general public.
    • Organize or support Fair Trade Fairs for local Fair Trade businesses.
    • Organize or support Fair Trade events such as soccer and volleyball tournaments
    • Encourage other groups such as local school boards and city councils to adopt Ethical Purchasing policies, such as have been adopted by the cities of Vancouver and San Francisco and several Catholic school boards.

A resolution in favour of an Ethical Purchasing policy for churches, YMCAs and other community groups.

Whereas: (Name of Organization ) supports the building of awareness among our members and the community on issues related to sweatshop production facilities and the exploitation of vulnerable workers such as women, children and prison inmates and

Whereas: We wish to take positive action against the evils of child labour, child prostitution, slave labour and unregulated dangerous working conditions and 

Whereas: There are existing and improving methods of verifying producers who compete by exploiting their workers and 

Whereas: There are alternative methods of production to those of large corporations that control the supply and prices of goods and

Whereas: It is evident that pressure from institutional purchasers, their staff and members is an effective way to promote improved working conditions and wages and to end the exploitation of vulnerable workers such as women and children

Be it resolved that (name of organization) adopt a policy of purchasing goods wherever possible, that can be verified as being either Sweat Free or Fair Trade certified, that is, they are produced by worker owned co-operatives and companies that do not exploit their workers and that provide fair wages and safe, healthy working conditions.

How to Get a "Fair Trade - No Sweat" Policy at your School

Many high schools bulk-purchase clothing – school uniforms, team uniforms, band uniforms and/or staff uniforms. And other schools bulk purchase clothes bearing the school name and insignia. A great way to raise awareness about sweatshop conditions and the use of child labour is to promote developing an Ethical Purchasing policy, a "Fair Trade – No Sweat" policy. 

Fair Trade products are mostly agricultural such as tea, coffee, sugar and chocolate. However there are now other items such as Fair Trade sports balls and cut flowers available as well as handcrafts, household items, toys and gifts.. Schools and staff use can use most of these products and should be encouraged to look for these items.

Here’s how:

  1. Organize a Fair Trade - No Sweat committee: Potential members include: students and teachers from your school and other schools in your board, members of Student Council or other school groups, parents, school or board alumni, retired teachers, staff and members of organizations like Development and Peace, Maquila Solidarity Network, OXFAM, OECTA, etc. Likely you will initially be a small group, but continue to seek out new members.

    2.  
  2. Research and Develop allies: 
  • Check the labels of your school clothing and make sure the symbol of Transfair Canada is on all products which claim to be Fair Trade or free of child labour. Find out who makes the products and in what countries? 
  • Find out who’s responsible for policy decisions about uniforms, sports balls, coffee supplies etc – the school board? The school? The athletics department? If the school board makes the purchasing decisions or sets policies for such decisions (most likely), contact your local trustee. The trustee can tell you about the current policy (if there is one) regarding school uniforms, and the steps you need to take to change it. If your trustee isn’t very sympathetic, call another trustee who might be more helpful. 
  • Find out what other students have done and components of a good policy (www.maquilasolidarity.org and Hamilton initiative: wydnsi@yahoo.com). 
  • Seek out support for initiative:. e.g. student clubs, parent organizations, teacher and support staff unions, sympathetic school board members, Development and Peace members and local Fair Trade groups.
  1. Make a Presentation to the School Board: Request to speak at a Board meeting. Share with the trustees the importance of such things as child labour, safe working conditions and fair wages, full public disclosure and other components of a strong ethical purchasing policy, and recommend that the Board develop a policy and include members of your committee on the policy writing team. Share your passion for improving labour conditions and emphasize the power of the Board to make a difference. Provide an info package to Board members.

    2.  
  2. Participate in the Writing of the Fair Trade - No Sweat policy: One of the best strategies to ensuring a strong policy, is have representatives from your committee on the Board policy writing committee. While working on this committee, be sure to receive support from members of your committee and outside organizations with expertise as the language is critical to a strong policy and it can be a tough negotiation process. Stick to your principles.

    3.  
  3. Presentation of policy to the Board of Trustees: Prior to the meeting, get a copy of the proposed Ethical Purchasing policy. If the policy is weak, it is critical that you contact the trustees to explain to them the weaknesses and to suggest appropriate action (amendments, voting no, etc). Organize an urgent action campaign to lobby trustees to take action. Request an opportunity to speak at the Board meeting when the vote on the new policy will happen. Use this moment to reiterate publicly your opinion of policy.
Action Ideas
  • Ask students and teachers to sign a petition urging your school or school board to adopt an Ethical Purchasing Policy. 
  • Stage a Sweatshop Fashion Show at your school. 
  • Ask permission to put up a clothesline in a hallway with information about working conditions written on T-shirts or other clothing hung on the line. 
  • Arrange to set up a display or an information table on sweatshops and the No Sweat campaign. 
  • Make a flyer about sweatshops and Fair Trade and distribute it to students. Students at one school distributed a leaflet at a football game with a guide to referee signals on one side and information about Nike sweatshops on the other. 
  • Arrange to speak to students in other clubs and classes about the issues. Teachers can arrange talks about sweatshops during their classes.
  • Write articles for your school newspaper. 
  • Invite students to wear an armband, a No Sweat button or Fair Trade material to show their support. 
  • Show a video in your class about sweatshops or organize a school assembly with a video and speaker. 
  • Hold a school-wide vote on the policy and report the results to the school board, or do a survey of students’ opinions on the issue. 
  • Contact outside supporters – students at other schools, unions, religious groups, women’s groups – and ask them to write letters supporting your campaign. 
  • Join with others in making a presentation to your school administration or school board on sweatshop and child labour practices and how a Fair Trade - No Sweat policy will improve conditions. 

 

Teachers

Teachers are playing a key role in campaigns for Ethical Purchasing policies. 

Below are some suggestions for teachers: 
  • Incorporate sweatshop and Fair Trade issues into your curriculum. 
  • Help organize classroom talks or student assemblies. 
  • Provide support to students in researching companies and identifying decision-makers. 
  • Make contact with other teachers, staff and parents who might support an Ethical Purchasing policy. 
  • Work with other teachers and students to adapt the model Ethical Purchasing policy to your situation. 
  • Arrange meetings with sympathetic school board trustees. 
  • Involve your union and the support staff union in lobbying your school board.         Return.

Fredericton (Canada) YMCA – Purchasing guidelines.

Section 12.2 of the Orientation Handbook. "No Sweat Purchasing Guidelines"

It is in the interest of the Fredericton YMCA to purchase goods and services from responsible manufacturers that provide quality products and services at a competitive price; however the Fredericton YMCA does not want to do business with companies that compete by exploiting their workers.

There have been many recent reports of worker rights abuses and sweatshop conditions, such as poverty wages, excessive hours of work, discrimination, abusive treatment, child labor and failure to provide statutory benefits. It is evident that pressure from institutional purchasers such as the Fredericton YMCA is an effective way to combat sweatshop practices.

As an organization, the Fredericton YMCA chooses to allocate its purchasing dollars in order to enhance, rather than degrade, the economic and social well being of garment workers.

Therefore the Fredericton YMCA will make a conscious effort to ensure that all apparel and textile goods purchased by the Fredericton YMCA, whether produced in Canada or abroad, are manufactured in accordance with the local labor laws and the international labor standards of the International Labor Organization Conventions regarding wages, hours of work, workplace health and safety, forced labor, child labor and freedom of associations and collective bargaining and any other relevant conventions.

Prior to purchasing textile goods, the staff will affirm with the Administration Office that the vendor has signed the prepared document confirming their compliance with these conditions.

Contents of a typical School Board No Sweat Purchasing policy.

(Based on the policy of the Hamilton (Ontario) Catholic School Board.

Summary: A No Sweat purchasing policy requires licencees and/or apparel suppliers to provide verifiable assurances that their products are made under conditions that comply with international labour standards and local laws. There is recognition that no work place is perfect and that achieving and maintaining compliance with ethical licencing and purchasing policies is an ongoing process.

Sections:

1.  Compliance with national Labour Laws and International Standards.
                   Safe, Just and Healthy Employment Standards

2. Public Disclosure of Factory Locations
                  Prior to signing of any contract/licensing agreement, suppliers shall publicly disclose to the Board the location of all factories involved in the manufacture of the products to be supplied. This information is to be provided on the Board’s prescribed disclosure forms which in turn shall be considered public information.
                  The Board will ensure that the disclosed information will be available to the public upon request.

3. Internal Monitoring by the company:
                 Annual Public Reports
                 Suppliers should be required to provide annual, public reports on their progress in achieving compliance with the No Sweat policy. Reports should include:
-  information on the supplier’s monitoring and verification program;
- the name of the third-party auditing organization if they are using one;
- the findings of monitoring and third-party audits; and
- a summary of corrective action taken and efforts to promote sustainable solutions to persistent problems.

4. External Verification by a Third Party - Third Party Factory Monitoring

               Clothing suppliers should also be required to allow factory monitoring by acceptable third-party and independent monitors when there are complaints of workers’ rights abuses. The Board shall designate a third party, like WRC (Workers Rights Consortium), to carry out any investigation and report their findings to the Board. In addition, the Fair Labour Association (FLA) has a monitoring and reporting program that can be accessed.
                The findings of third-party audits are to be made available to the institution, the people making the complaint, and the public.

5. Corrective Action.
                Few, if any, factories are totally free from violations and the institution must be prepared to receive complaints. Manufacturers should be given sufficient time and opportunities to achieve compliance. The supplier should provide the Institution with a corrective action plan, including reasonable deadlines. The institution has the authority to terminate the contract if the supplier does not comply.
               The Board will exercise its mandate to insist that corrective action be taken by the company in the shortest period of time possible but not longer than 6 months. If the supplier/licensee fails and/or refuses to rectify the concerns to the satisfaction of the Board’s designate, the Board or school principal shall terminate the contract.

6. Responding to Complaints
              Suppliers should be required to respond to all legitimate complaints with verified evidence that the violations have either not occurred or corrective action has been taken.

7. Compliance Form
              Suppliers should be required to complete and sign a compliance form that describes and details all the expectations the institution’s policy enforces as a condition of business.

8. Annual letter to uniform suppliers

9. List of approved suppliers
             At the beginning of each March the Board shall make known to all schools through the principal who have met requirements of this policy.

From: Michael Zelmer of the Vancouver Fair Trade Coffee Network. February, 2005.

The Ethical Purchasing Policy (EPP) and Supplier Code of Conduct (SCC) for the City of Vancouver and other partners passed the final hurdle recently.
To get the final version of the SCC and EPP, as well as the report by staff on the process and costs of the policy, (and a video clip on the meeting) follow this link:

http://vancouver.ca/ctyclerk/cclerk/20050217/cs20050217.htm (It's item #7) 

This policy sets out the ethical requirements for all City purchases of apparel and "Fair Trade agricultural products". In a nutshell, the City can't buy apparel that doesn't meet the International Labour Organizations' (ILO) core conventions, and must buy Fair Trade certified agricultural products whenever possible. "Whenever possible" means subject to supply... so since the FT certified coffee market can easily supply the City's needs, all City coffee will be FT certified. Other products (like rice, for instance), may require more time.

With the decision to allow for only FT certified products, in order to have the City commit to buying FT products, we needed to present them with a system in which they could feel confident that they weren't going to get bitten in the ass. By only allowing FT certified goods, the City wouldn't be responsible for costly (time, money, and personel) auditing of supply chains, since that would be handled by Transfair/FLO. As well, if there was ever a scandal involving a certified product that wasn't as ethical as it seemed, the City would have some distance from it since they would have been relying on an internationally recognized system (i.e. the problem would have been with FLO/Transfair if such a situation ever arose).

In Vancouver, there are 4 non-certified coffee "brands" of importance: Development and Peace, Cafe Etico, Cafe Justicia, and Level Ground Trading. Of those, one is now partly certified and increasing certification for the rest of its product lines, another is in the process of being certified, and the other two aren't certified yet. The fact that EVERY roaster makes the claim that they're ethical (never mind suppliers of other products like tea, chocolate, sugar, fruit, etc), and that we couldn't be sure that the City would be able to acquire the knowledge necessary to adequately screen bogus claims, insisting on certification was the best way to go.

Staff estimated total costs to the City to be just below of $600 000 for the coming year (including additional staff requirements and one time consultancy fees for implementation purposes) (see report). The report makes no investigation of the City's ability to recover/reduce some of these costs through adjusting retail prices at concession stands, or the quantity of these goods that it consumes.

What’s the difference between No Sweat and Fair Trade campaigns?
From Dave Hall, Y FOCUS Fair Trade Sports
Both campaigns are aimed at improving wages, health and safety in the workplace, ending the exploitation of women and children and providing workers with an active role in the decisions made in the work place and community.

No Sweat campaigns are largely directed at large companies, selling clothing and accessories, where the work is outsourced to factories scattered throughout developing countries where labour is cheap and plentiful. Third party monitoring and reporting are carried out by such groups as the Workers Rights Consortium and the Fair Labour Association.

Fair Trade campaigns tend to focus on smaller producers, often locally owned farmer and worker owned co-operatives as the alternative to large corporations who might dominate and control a particular segment of the industry such as bananas or cocoa. Most Fair Trade products are agricultural but the list has expanded to include handcrafts, toys, sports balls and cut flowers. Fair Trade producers and manufacturers are certified, licenced and monitored by the Fairtrade Labeling organization based in Germany. In the countries where Fair Trade products are sold, importers are certified by FLO and the corresponding national Fair Trade organization such as Transfair Canada.
