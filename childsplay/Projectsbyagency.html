ACCESO INTERNATIONAL

HAITI

ACCESO International supports a homework centre in Verettes and a primary school in Gonaïves. The homework centre in Verettes offers literacy support for at-risk, impoverished and orphaned children. Activities include tutoring sessions, basic literacy and pre-school classes, as well as summer camps. The intention is to enable these children to grow as persons who are free, responsible, builders of peace and committed to changing their world so that they will be able to actively shape their own lives and to experience all the rights to which they are entitled. At the primary school in Gonaïves, ACCESO International is providing educational supplies and a much needed meal program for over 700 children who attend daily classes in three different shifts. ACCESO International is assisting this school in its efforts to rebuild after it was severely damaged by a hurricane in 2004.

HONDURAS

ACCESO International provides educational materials and bursary assistance for an orphanage in La Flecha, an orphanage in El Progreso, a primary school in the remote mountain village of Buen Ejemplo and a home work centre in Los Laureles. Through these four projects, over 400 primary level children are being individually

assisted with shoes, packsacks, uniforms and nutrition, while their classrooms have become more colourful, creative and stimulating with many much needed school supplies. ACCESO International also helps a high school in El Progreso and a high school in La Lima whereby 3 young women are receiving full bursaries to attend these institutions and the classrooms and libraries of over 1500 high school students are being

filled with supplies and books.

Childhaven International.

NEPAL

Childhaven International operate an orphanage in Arubari, a small village about 8km outside of Kathmandu. The orphanage is run by Nepali staff and volunteers from all over the world. See www.childhaven.ca for more information.

There are over 100 boys and girls who live full-time at the orphanage in Arubari. They range in age from 1 year to 18 years although the average age is probably about 8 years old. Not all of the children are orphans but they are all children who were not consistently receiving at least one meal a day before they were taken into the home. At the home they receive 3 meals a day which consists of mostly rice, curried potatoes, lentils and a few vegetables. They also have a machine that produces soy milk from soy beans and each child gets a glass of soy milk twice a day. The home is a modern design and equipped with electric lights, plumbing and a solar hot water heater but a low water table has left the well dry so water must be trucked in a few times a week. The children sleep on the floor, about 10 to a room that is roughly the size of an average North American bedroom.
 
 

PROJECT HELP LESOTHO

LESOTHO

Help Lesotho is a Canadian initiative partnering public, private and volunteer resources, as a response to the increasingly alarming effects of poverty and HIV/AIDS in Lesotho, Southern Africa.

Project Help Lesotho was created in 2004 by an Ottawa resident, Dr. Peg Herbert. Peg was inspired by a friend of hers from Lesotho who was studying at Ottawa University. Lesotho is a small, land locked country that is completely surrounded by South Africa. The people are very poor and the country is being devastated by the HIV/AIDS pandemic. Project Help Lesotho has spear headed many programs to help the Basotho children in the following ways: the building and supporting of boarding schools for young orphan girls, the twinning of Canadian and Besotho schools to promote friendship, give financial support to the schools in Lesotho and to help purchase school uniforms for the children. There are also some economic projects that support local women making bead broaches, canvas bags and hats. There have been leadership camps for the Besotho school children as well as leadership awards given to worthy recipients. Canadians are given the opportunity to sponsor orphans, ensuring they have a school uniform and that their fees are paid so they can attend school.

There are now over 6,000 Basotho (the name of the people of Lesotho) children benefiting from our projects. There are thousands of Canadian students writing to their pen pals in English, their second language. In Canada, hundreds are involved in various ways. Funds are raised by children for children. Schools, churches, and individuals have come forward with real compassion to form relationships with our Basotho friends. Many have volunteered to work in Lesotho on specific projects for 1-6 months. In Lesotho, there are over 20 projects underway, with more in the final planning stages.

One of the poorest countries in the world, Lesotho has the 3rd highest rate of HIV/AIDS on earth. Over 30% of children are orphaned and life expectancy is rapidly falling to 31 years. Over 60% of the population is under 24 years of age. With a rapidly decreasing population of 2.1 million and a landmass of 30,500 sq. km, it is an unusually homogeneous population of 99.7% Basotho people, of whom 80% are Christian. It therefore does not have the racial and ethnic conflicts that draw media attention.
Called Roof of Africa by virtue of being the third highest country in the world, Lesotho is a small, mountainous and deforested country, with almost no natural resources. Well into its 5th year of debilitating drought, there is also a crucial water and food shortage. Soil erosion is expected to deplete arable land by 2040. An unemployment rate reaching 50-60% and an alarming degree of crime and stock theft are producing an unprecedented state of poverty.

Salv-Aide.

EL SALVADOR.

Their mission is to build social and economic justice, democracy and dignity with the people of El Salvador. SalvAide is a registered Canadian charitable organization composed of a network of communities and supporters that stretches from coast to coast.

SALV AIDE'S GOALS ARE:

1.  To increase the strength of civil society in El Salvador as represented by non- governmental

 organizations (NGOs), popular organizations, women's organizations and communities.

2.  To strengthen local economies in El Salvador which are participating in regional and national

efforts to create sustainable and diversified economic development.

3.  To build Canadian support for community and democratic development in El Salvador.

 SALVAIDE  activities:

SalvAide funds development projects in El Salvador designed by rural communities to meet their needs and priorities. Since 1985 SalvAide has funded such diverse projects as poultry farms, dairy herds, daycares, schools, community stores, medical clinics, cottage industries and legal aid clinics. SalvAide also funds human rights work and organizational development of Salvadorean non-governmental and popular organizations.
 
 

St Mary’s Anglican Church in Richmond Hill, Ontario .

ZAMBIA.

The Kalingalinga Orphanage feeds 500 kids daily many positive for AIDS.. It is run by a Bishop Anderson. The Kanyama Community School, a non-government school, has over 300 students with 5 non-paid teachers and about 30% of the children are infected. Mostly single and double-orphans (1 or both parents died).

Training Centre in Livingstone: Fr. Katete Jackosn Jones .

We are now partnering with ILO in withdrawing and preventing children from involvement in child labour in Livingstone. They are targeting 400 children to benefit. 200 will be taken out of child labour while 200 will be prevented from going into child labour. Of the 200 to be withdrawn, 100 will be trained at our Centre for survival skills such as Carpentry, Tailoring , Tie & Dye and hair dressing.

Most of these children are exploited in such things as prostitution (Sex workers), crushing stones, selling different things in the streets, domestic works (servants or baby sitters), charcoal burning, etc. They go into all jobs so that they pay for schooling and to buy food because their parents have died and the guardian cannot afford to provide everything they may need. Sometimes it is the guardian or single parent that would send them to work to make ends meet.

At our training centre we have a limited capacity to accommodate the many children who may need these skills. We have negotiated with government that the children we shall be sponsoring should be exempted from school fees as a contribution from government.

We believe that in this way, we shall put to an end to the vicious cycle of HIV/AIDS and poverty and the children shall be empowered with skills to survive and with formal education for their future.
 
 

SIDA/AIDS Moncton & AIDS Saint John in New Brunswick

SWAZILAND

They have partnered with The AIDS Information and Support Centre in Swaziland. This small African country has the highest HIV infection rate in the world - 42.6%. The number of orphans continues to rise and is currently at 69,000 in a population of 1 million. During our recent visit to Swaziland in April/May 2005, the Vunca Sincobe Youth Association pointed out their need for soccer equipment in order to provide physical activity for the youth in the Village of Nkamazi.

Through Canadian Crossroads International, the two organizations are trying to address the impact of HIV/AIDS in the rural Swazi community, as well as helping strengthen the capacity of a local Swazi organization. In the village, Nkamazi, they are working with the leadership of the Youth Association, and a group of peer helpers called the 'Sizanani

Support Group'. Their request for help included a request for soccer balls and shoes for the children of the village. The community is already working on a project to grow vegetables for orphans in the village, as well as a potable water project; and are exploring other ways of helping orphans and vulnerable children in the community especially using sports such as soccer.

Swaziland is named by UNAIDS as the country with the highest rate of HIV infection - in the most recent survey 42.6% of pregnant women were HIV positive. The number of orphans in our partner village is growing at an astounding rate - in six months of last year

the number of orphans grew from 77 to 110 (among a total population of 1800).
 

SOS Children’s Villages Canada.

BRAZIL

SOS Children's Villages has been in existence for more than 50 years and has become active in 131 countries. Girls and boys find a new home in more than 440 SOS Children's Villages - children who have lost their parents through war, disease or natural disaster, who have been forced out of their homes or who can no longer live with their parents.

SOS Children’s Villages in Brazil
With the aim of providing support to the poorer members of society, SOS Children's Villages started working in Brazil in the late 1960s, opening three SOS Children's Villages in Brasilia, Porto Alegre and Poà near Sao Paulo in 1970. The villages were able to provide new homes and security children whose parents could no longer support them during the difficult economic situation at the time. Since then a further eleven SOS Children's Villages have been built, in addition to thirteen SOS Youth facilities, twelve SOS Social Centres and four SOS Hermann-Gmeiner Schools in order to provide the education and skills necessary for a brighter future.

Recife is a large town, which has grown quickly and without careful planning, leading to the concentration of social problems in the area. It has a population of almost 1.5 million, of which a quarter is under the age of 15 and a third is living on less than half of the minimum salary (Foundation Getulio Vargas). School attendance rates are particularly alarming, with 280,000 people, or 20% of the population, having less than one year's schooling and over 200,000 people have only completed up to three years of education. Almost 10% of the town's population is estimated to be unemployed, with the informal sector providing employment for substantial proportion of the population.

A recent UNESCO map of violence identified Pernambuco as being amongst the worst states for youth violence in Brazil, with Recife having the second highest murder rate of young people and worrying statistics relating to drugs and sexual exploitation. Over 300 children and young people in the area are believed to be in need of long term support and assistance, but for whom, because of their age, ethnic background or other reason, adoption is highly unlikely. Official social provisions for these children and youths are increasingly inappropriate with numbers of places constantly reduced, often resulting in their relocation to institutions in neighbouring cities away from their family and friends. Such social, economic and political factors have therefore convinced the local authorities of the need for an SOS Children's Village in the area.
 

Y FOCUS_Ottawa and the Freetown Y Service Club.

SIERRA LEONE

The Freetown Y Service club with the support of Y FOCUS is implementing a training program for unemployed young adults, where they learn about indigenous products they can make and receive basic business training so that they can form co-operatives to provide them with jobs and incomes. 56 young people in 8 communities are being trained during the first year of the program and will be helped to set up their own business usually as a co-operative in groups of 4-5 youth.
 

 There is also an orphanage called Jonathan’s House supported by the Y Service club and the YMCA in Freetown. The home is committed to finding kids on the streets and providing food, clothing, shelter, education and medical  treatment.  The children’s fees and uniforms for school are paid for and the YMCA in  Freetown provides administrative support and oversight. There are 62 children in the program, boys and girls of all ages with a budget of $35 000 per year.
 
 

Wakefield Grannies.

SOUTH AFRICA.

In a poor township, there are many grandmothers now left with grandchildren to look after since their parents have died.
The Wakefield Grannies.  The Wakefield Grannies are a group of women offering support to AIDs Orphans and their Grandmothers in Alexandra Township, South Africa by providing direct financial assistance to the Tara Hospital Children's Clinic, and by keeping a correspondence with the individual women who attend the clinic’s program.

The Background
On a visit to Quebec in 2004, Rose Letwaba spoke about her work to a small audience in the Wakefield United Church.  Rose is a nurse in the health clinic in Alexandra Township on the outskirts of Johannesburg, South Africa.  The picture she painted was of a whole generation of South Africans lost to AIDs and grieving mothers left to carry the burden of raising their grandchildren to be healthy, educated and socially responsible adults.  Rose described a group of 40 such Grannies who were meeting at her clinic for sewing classes, gardening and moral support.

Spontaneously, 12 Wakefield women, lead by Norma Geggie (81) came together to help, becoming the first Grannies to link with the Alexandra Grannies or GoGos to use the South African word.

Today
The Wakefield Grannies are committed to providing financial assistance to their counterparts in Alexandra. To do this they produce fundraising events.
Each Wakefield Granny also has an individual GoGo to whom they write letters.  It may seem that raising money is the most important aspect of their activities.  In fact, Rose Letwaba wrote after some South African GoGos received letters from Canada that “Morale is high in these groups and there is a lot of hope, just the idea and the thought that there are Grannies on the other side of the world who care so much about them, make these groups appreciate life…”
 
More formation on the Wakefield Grannies can be found on their Internet site: www.thegrannies.org
