SOS Children’s Villages in Brazil

With the aim of providing support to the poorer members of society, SOS Children's Villages started working in Brazil in the late 1960s, opening three SOS Children's Villages in Brasilia, Porto Alegre and Poà near Sao Paulo in 1970. The villages were able to provide new homes and security children whose parents could no longer support them during the difficult economic situation at the time. Since then a further eleven SOS Children's Villages have been built, in addition to thirteen SOS Youth facilities, twelve SOS Social Centres and four SOS Hermann-Gmeiner Schools in order to provide the education and skills necessary for a brighter future.

The '6 villages for 2006' Village

Brazil is home to vast numbers of the population living on or below the poverty line. A large percentage of these poor are concentrated in the North East of the country, where social provisions are scarce. The coastal town of Recife, in the North Eastern province of Pernambuco, has therefore been chosen as one of the FIFA/SOS "6 villages for 2006". The campaign, a joint project between world football's governing body, FIFA, and SOS Children's Villages will run until the end of the 2006 FIFA World Cup and aims to raise funds for six new SOS Children's Villages in order to provide more disadvantaged children with a new home and hope for the future

Recife is a large town, which has grown quickly and without careful planning, leading to the concentration of social problems in the area. It has a population of almost 1.5 million, of which a quarter is under the age of 15 and a third is living on less than half of the minimum salary (Foundation Getulio Vargas). School attendance rates are particularly alarming, with 280,000 people, or 20% of the population, having less than one year's schooling and over 200,000 people have only completed up to three years of education. Almost 10% of the town's population is estimated to be unemployed, with the informal sector providing employment for substantial proportion of the population.

A recent UNESCO map of violence identified Pernambuco as being amongst the worst states for youth violence in Brazil, with Recife having the second highest murder rate of young people and worrying statistics relating to drugs and sexual exploitation. Over 300 children and young people in the area are believed to be in need of long term support and assistance, but for whom, because of their age, ethnic background or other reason, adoption is highly unlikely. Official social provisions for these children and youths are increasingly inappropriate with numbers of places constantly reduced, often resulting in their relocation to institutions in neighbouring cities away from their family and friends. Such social, economic and political factors have therefore convinced the local authorities of the need for an SOS Children's Village in the area.

A 30,000m² area of land in the Igarassu district, 26km from the centre of Recife, is to be donated by the State of Pernambuco. Construction of the twelve new family houses, ancillary buildings and sports ground will commence in April 2005, and should be completed for the arrival of the 108 intended children by December 2006. The facilities will also include a kindergarten, with 2 classes, and a social centre offering medical and dental assistance as well as counselling and psychological support. The proposed site is ideally positioned for local amenities, with large shopping centres and markets, banks, schools, health centres and a number of other businesses nearby as well as good public transport links.

A snapshot of the country.

Covering an area of more than 8.5 million km², Brazil is the fifth largest country in the world and by far the largest country in South America. It is home to a wealth of natural resources and some of the world's most diverse flora and fauna, including the Amazon rainforest, which contains 30% of the world's remaining forest (Lonely Planet, 2004). The country also ranks among the world's top ten economies, its Gross Domestic Product exceeding USD 1.379 trillion (CIA Factbook 2004). However, with a population of over 176 million, a quarter of which currently lives on USD 1 a day (DFID 2004), Brazil's vast wealth is among the most unevenly distributed in the world.

The only South American nation to have been under the rule of the Portuguese, Brazil gained independence in 1822, following which the country has seen a number of military interventions in the government, resulting in a largely authoritarian rule before a new, more democratic constitution was introduced in 1988. Despite enjoying periods of rapid economic growth, and the development of large and profitable agricultural, mining, manufacturing and service sectors, the country has suffered from years of corruption and economic neglect.

Due to the country's rapidly increasing population growth and the significant rural migration, Brazil is home to some of the largest cities in the world, with roughly 80% of the country's population living in urban areas (USAID 2004). Unemployment in urban areas is estimated to be as high as 25%, almost double the national average (USAID 2004), and consequently poverty in the towns and cities is considerable. Brazil's cities are indeed famous for their sprawling shanty towns, or favelas, which are often found in areas considered unsuitable for habitation due to adverse geographical conditions and lack of resources and public services, but where the country's poor are forced to seek refuge.

Because of the extreme poverty in urban areas, many children and youths are forced to work or beg for a living rather than attend school. Some children, whose families can no longer support them, find themselves living on the streets exposed to drugs and crime and even physical and sexual abuse.
